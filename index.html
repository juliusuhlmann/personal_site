On Building Taste Through Repetition

April 12, 2026 6 min read

Repetition is not the boring part of getting better. It is the mechanism that lets your standards become precise enough to trust.

Most people treat taste as something mystical. Either you have it or you do not. In practice, taste usually looks much less glamorous. It comes from seeing enough attempts in the same category that small differences stop feeling small.

The first ten versions of a thing mostly teach you what obvious mistakes look like. The next twenty teach you what almost-working feels like.

Somewhere after that, you begin to recognize which details are carrying the whole experience and which ones are only noise.

What repetition changes

Repetition compresses feedback. Instead of reacting to a finished outcome, you start noticing quality while you are still making the thing. That is when your decisions get faster and sharper.

The point is not to repeat mechanically. The point is to repeat attentively enough that your eye becomes difficult to fool.

This is why a strong creative practice often looks narrow from the outside. People revisit the same page layout, the same sentence rhythm, the same training loop, the same interface pattern. They are not stuck. They are training discrimination.

A Small System for Better Notes

October 3, 2025 4 min read

Good notes should reduce friction twice: once when you capture the thought, and once again when you need it back.

A lot of note-taking systems become miniature bureaucracies. They ask for tags, folders, statuses, backlinks, templates, and some ongoing act of classification before an idea is even allowed to exist.

A smaller system usually works better. Capture quickly. Rewrite only what still feels alive the next day. Promote only the pieces that keep proving useful. Most notes do not deserve permanence.

Three layers are enough

I like a simple split between fleeting notes, working notes, and durable notes. Fleeting notes are raw. Working notes are where ideas collide.

Durable notes are compressed conclusions you would actually want to meet again in six months.

The key is that each layer should demand more clarity than the one before it. That way the archive naturally gets smaller as its value goes up.

Why I Keep Returning to Clean Interfaces

September 18, 2025 5 min read

Clean interfaces are not interesting because they are minimal. They are interesting because they remove excuses for unclear thinking.

Whenever an interface feels calm, people assume the work was mostly visual. Often the real effort happened in prioritization. The design looks simple because someone kept refusing to let low-value elements compete with the primary task.

Clean does not mean empty. It means every visible decision is carrying weight. The layout tells you what matters first. The type scale tells you what is supporting material.

The spacing tells you which objects belong together.

Restraint creates speed

People move faster in interfaces that do not constantly ask them to re-evaluate where attention should go. The best products feel almost unfair in this way. They make good decisions look obvious.

That is why I keep coming back to restraint. Not because it is a style, but because it is one of the clearest signs that the designer understood the job.
